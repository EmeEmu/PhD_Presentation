During my PhD, I've been interested in understanding how to compare the brain activity of multiple individuals, and in particular, how to compare this activity when the people are not doing anything, and not thinking about anything in particular.

Introduction

  • Gordon & Laumann et al Neuron 2017

Traditionally in neuroscience, when we want to learn something about how the brain works, we usually record the brain of participants while asking them to do something. So for example, in humans, you might record their brains using FMRI while they are watching a movie, moving their hands or feets, or whatever.

Then you deduce what parts of the brain are related to, for example, moving their feet. But this only gives you information on that particular person. To learn something general about the human brain, we have to do the same experiment in many other participants and average their maps to get the brain regions common to everyone.

However, this type of analysis doesn't work if you are interested in the brain activity which is not evoked by a particular behaviour or stimulus.

Introduction

Spontaneous Brain Activity

Fundamental feature of Brain Function

⠀

linked with Brain Structure

This type of activity is called spontaneous brain activity, which is often described as ongoing and structured brain activity, which is unrelated to a current task or an explicit sensory input. Now I'll try and decompose this definition a little bit.

“Ongoing” means that it is constantly present, when you are sleeping, when you are anaesthetised, when your mind is wandering. But, even when you are actively doing something. But the easiest way to observe it is at rest, when participants are asked to do nothing.

The second part is structured. This is going to be a big part of this presentation, so I won't go into too much detail now, but the idea is that spontaneous brain activity is not randomness. It follows particular structures in the brain and it resembles normal brain activity.

The last part means that this activity is not driven by a task or a stimulus. So when you average across trials or across individuals this activity will disappear. Because it’s an activity which isn’t reproducible. This is why studying SA, particularly across individuals, is such a challenge.

Now, you might be wondering why we even bother with SA if it’s not related to doing stuff. It’s very important because it is involved in a whole bunch of different brain functions. For example, during development, both before and after you are born, SA plays a major role in shaping the structure of your brain. In recent years, some people have started to study how abnormal SA can be a marker for some psychiatric disorders like autism for example.

And it's extremely involved in memory and learning as I'll show you in a bit, as well as in behaviour, which can be partially driven by SA.

The fundamental idea is that spontaneous brain activity reflects the structure of your brain, and in particular how the different parts of your brains are connected with each other.

Introduction

Spontaneous Activity reflects Brain Structure

Spontaneous Activity = intrinsic revisiting of the network

⠀

⠀

⇒ reflects network structure

If you look at the structure of a brain, you'll see that there are brain regions which are more or less connected with each other. Some of them (in red) will be exciting each other, others will be inhibiting each other. And you have this very particular type of connectivity where not everyone is connected to everyone, but you have hubs and recurrent connections. If you look at the brain in more detail, at the much smaller scale of neurons, you're going to see that those circuits of neurons are also connected in this way with groups of neurons that are very strongly connected and then send connections to other groups and circuits that are further away. These small groups of neurons which are tightly connected, are called cell assemblies. And the idea is that throughout your experience, those neurons have learned to activate or deactivate altogether.

Spontaneous activity happens like all brain activity in this structured network and therefore has to follow the rules of that network. As physicists we like to think about this in terms of energy. The brain can be in different states or configurations, which each correspond to a possible activity pattern of the whole brain. Some configurations would have higher energy, others low energy, and the brain evolves in this landscape by falling into the valleys or jumping over the hills. But you can imagine that some trajectories are easier to follow than others.

The idea is that SA revisits this landscape without the biases or constraints that would be induced by a task or a stimulus. And therefore it’s helpful if we want to study how the brain is intrinsically organized.

Introduction

Spontaneous Activity Revisits the Network

  • Gupta et al Neuron 2010

An example of this I really like is an experiment in how rats move through a maze. So you have this maze where a rat has to follow those arrows so only experiences the maze in one direction.

At the same time, it has a microscope attached to its head which records the activity of special neurons called place cells that only activate when the rat is at a particular location in the maze. So for example, the red place cell will only activate when the rat is at the top right corner of the maze. And so from the activity of those neurons, you can decode where the rat is according to its brain.

During periods when that rat is immobile and not doing anything, SA revisits those place cells as if the rat was really moving around. Some trajectories look like what the rat has experienced, so in the direction of the arrows. Other trajectories are in reverse. But amazingly, some trajectories are shortcuts that were never experienced by the rat.

So again, the idea is that SA is a way of revisiting brain networks, but it’s not just a simple replay of past experiences.

Stereotypy of Network Structure across Individuals

Shared componants of brain structure ⇒ Shared componants of Spontaneous Activity

  • Gratton et al Neuron 2018

So if we go back to our question of comparing the spontaneous brain activity of multiple individuals, maybe you see now that if the structure of the brain is the same for two individuals, then we expect them to have roughly the same spontaneous activity.

This is something that has been tested in the past where people have looked at how the different parts of the brain are connected and have tried to deduce how much of this structure is shared amongst multiple individuals, how much is specific to each individual, and then a few other things around whether those networks change depending on the task.

And what they have found is that most of the structure is either specific to an individual or shared by all the group, and those two components are about half and half. This means that a very large component of the brain structure is shared, and therefore we should have components of spontaneous activity which are also shared among individuals.

Restricted Boltzmann Machines

A Probabilistic Model

Geoffrey Hinton

Instead I’ve been using a mathematical model of this demon, which is called the Restricted Boltzmann Machine which has been largely developed by Geoffrey Hinton.

The idea behind this model is to have two layers, one which represents the neurons on the left and green, where each neuron can be either on or off, and one on the right that represents hidden units, which are equivalent to the arms of the demon, and will provide a representation of the brain activity.

Mathematically, the model is described by a joint probability distribution over the neuron and hidden unit configurations. It's a boltman distribution defined from an energy function with 3 terms.

The first term describes the tendency of each neuron to be ON or OFF.

The second term describes how each hidden unit can be more or less active, but with continuous values.

And the last term describes the interactions between the neurons and the hidden units, mediated by weights that connect directly the neurons and the hidden units. So each weight in a continuous value, which can be positive of negative

So the entire model is parametrized by those 3 parameters g and U which I won’t discuss much here, and the weights w_i_mu which we’ll discuss in length throughout this presentation.

Restricted Boltzmann Machines

A Generative Model

maximize $\mathcal{L} = \langle \log P(\mathbf{v}) \rangle_\text{data}$
learn parameters $ \begin{cases} &g_i\\ &\mathcal{U}_\mu\\ &w_{i\mu} \end{cases} $

The goal of this model is to capture the distribution of neuronal configurations P(v), and so the model is trained by modifying the parameters to maximize the likelihood of the data. However, it’s impossible to compute P(v) directly.

But we are saved by the fact that this model is generative. The idea is that the joint distribution P(v,h) can be decomposed into 2 conditional distributions which allow us to move between the visible and hidden representations of the data. So for example, we can start from this brain configuration, where some neurons are active and some neurons are inactive, and then use P(h|v) to generate a hidden representation. We can also do the same the other way around, using P(v|h) to generate a visible configuration from this hidden representation.

By repeating this procedure over and over again we can generate a whole bunch of new configurations which should look like the genuine recorded brain activity, meaning that they follow P(v).

Representing Brain Activity as a Composition of Cell Assemblies

Thijs van der Plas

Jérôme Tubiana

  • van der Plas & Tubiana et al. eLife 2023

Amongst many other things, they have shown that the RBM provides a representation of brain activity as a composition of cell assemblies. I'm going to unpack this statement because it's an extremely important one for this presentation.

The idea is that RBMs capture the co-activation motifs of neurons by grouping them to the same hidden units. So if we imagine that those five neurons have a tendency of always being active together, then they will all be grouped to this blue hidden unit.

And it's the same for other groups of neurons that also tend to co-activate, and each of them will be linked to a hidden unit.

And so this creates groups of neurons, throughout the brains, that are each linked to a different hidden unit.

An important result from Thijs and Jerome is that these groups describe populations of neurons which are known and look like known functional circuits. For example, this one here looks a lot like pre-motor neurons that control the movement of the tail.

An important feature of RBMs is that one neuron can be connected to more than 1 hidden unit, like this one for example.

This overlap combined with the fact that weights can have positive and negative values means that we can compose a large variety of states.

Here I'm showing you a toy example with 3 hidden units. Each dot is a neuron and the color shows how they connect to each hidden unit, with blue for negative weights, red for positive weights, and white when they're not connected.

So hidden units don’t represent brain states, a brain state is a composition of a few hidden units.

Restricted Boltzmann Machines

Provide Degenerate Representations

Goal : Find a Common Representation for the Spontaneous Brain Activity of multiple fish.

Now, our goal is to find a representation of brain activity which is shared amongst many fish, not just 1.

But it seems that RBMs are quite good candidates for doing this as hidden units capture functional networks which we know are shared across fish.

So the idea would be to train 1 RBM per fish, with the hope that their respective hidden units describe the same neuronal populations.

However, even if those 2 fish were identical, there is a risk that the 2 RBMs would converge to different representations, as the training algorithm of RBMs is stochastic.

To test this, we did a simple experiment where we trained two RBMs on the same dataset. And then we've compared the representation of this activity by looking at how correlated were the weight vectors to each hidden unit in the two models.

So on the X axis here you have the hidden units of one training, and on the Y axis you have the hidden units of the other training and the colour describes their correlation with each other.

If those 2 RBMs represented the activity in the same way, the diagonal would be entirely red. But we see that this is not the case. Around 35% of those hidden units have no matching pair. Then you have 40% of those hidden units where it's not very clear whether they match or not, but you still have around 30% that match perfectly. So one hidden unit in one brain will match perfectly.

So this means that 2 models trained on exactly the same data can converge to 2 different representations of this activity. And so there is no way that this naive approach would work across multiple individuals.

Aligning Representation Across Individuals

Hypothesis :

⠀

⠀

cell assemblies are shared across individuals

⠀

⠀

The hypothesis on which this rests is that you can find the same cell assemblies in all the individuals and that they are located in the same place in the brain.

And so what we are going to do is train one model per fish and then constrain those models using this spatial prior such that they have the same hidden unit representation.

Aligning Representation Across Individuals

Method : Latent-aligned RBMs

So the method goes like this. We start by training a first RBM on 1 fish like Thijs and Jerome have done before. We call this RBM a teacher, and we will use it to constrain a student RBMs trained on a second fish.

The first step is to initialise this student.

We know that each hidden unit maps to certain brain regions with certain weights, and so we can map this spatial distribution into the student fish, and use it to reconstruct, in the student, the weights to that particular hidden unit, which means that it now represents the same neuronal population in both fish.

And we do this for every hidden unit to reconstruct a full weight matrix. And so with this initialization method, we have built a student RBM, which should have the same representation space as the teacher.

However, at this point the student is not at all specialized to the second fish, which is its own individual and therefore needs to be trained.

Normally, we would train it by maximazing the likelihood of the neuronal data from the second fish.

But if we do this it will slowly drift away from the teacher representation which we don't want.

With Jorge we developped a method where we also constrain the student from the teacher model by generating hidden configuration from the teacher and training the student RBM to maximise the probability of those configuration.

And we mix those two training objectives into one so the student learns to reproduce both the student neuronal configurations and the teacher hidden cofigurations.

What we found is that this training paradigm works extremely well. We can train the student RBMs about 10 times faster than we would the teacher RBMs and they converge way more reliably, meaning that they almost systematically provide good solutions to the problem.
